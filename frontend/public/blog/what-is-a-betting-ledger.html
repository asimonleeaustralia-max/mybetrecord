What Is a Betting Ledger?

A betting ledger is a structured record of every wager you place. Learn why disciplined bettors keep one and what to track.

A betting ledger is simply a complete, chronological record of your wagers: what you backed, at what odds, for how much, and how it settled. Think of it as the difference between guessing whether you are up for the season and knowing.

Why spreadsheets fail

Most bettors start in Excel or Google Sheets. That works until:

  • Formulas break when you add each-way or exchange commission columns
  • You forget to log a void or cash-out
  • You cannot slice by tipster or sport without rebuilding pivot tables

A dedicated ledger normalises odds, recomputes profit on every edit, and keeps reports consistent.

What to record

At minimum, log:

  1. Event and selection — enough detail to recognise the bet months later
  2. Stake and currency
  3. Odds (in whatever format the book displays)
  4. Outcome — pending, win, loss, void
  5. Settled profit — or let software derive it from outcome rules

Advanced fields that separate sharp trackers from casual note-takers:

  • Personal implied odds (your fair price)
  • Closing line odds (for CLV)
  • Tipster or model source
  • Bookmaker and commission

Ledger vs journal

People use “betting journal” and “betting ledger” interchangeably. Strictly, a journal might include narrative notes (“felt rushed”, “line moved”); a ledger emphasises numbers. The best tools combine both: structured data plus free-text notes per bet.

Who needs one?

Anyone who treats betting as a skill game rather than lottery tickets:

  • Matched bettors tracking qualifying and free bets
  • Exchange traders measuring commission-adjusted yield
  • Tipster followers verifying advertised ROI
  • Recreational punters who want honest bankroll management

Getting started

Pick one sport or market type and log the next 20 bets without changing your process. Review strike rate, average odds, and profit by week. That baseline is worth more than any tip.
